The Old System — What the Global Package Was
30+ years of bundled OB billing explained
▼
Core concept for your script: Since the early 1990s, a single code could bill for an entire pregnancy — every prenatal visit, labor, delivery, and postpartum care all rolled into one. That system ends January 1, 2027.

What is a Global Package?

A "global package" is a single CPT code that covers all physician services for a defined episode of care — in this case, the full continuum of a pregnancy. From the first prenatal visit through the final postpartum visit, one code triggered one payment.

The four most common global OB codes were:

CodeDescriptionWhat It Covered
59400Routine obstetric care — vaginal deliveryAll antepartum visits + vaginal delivery + postpartum care
59510Routine obstetric care — cesarean deliveryAll antepartum visits + cesarean delivery + postpartum care
59610Routine OB care — VBACAntepartum visits + VBAC attempt + postpartum care
59618Routine OB care — VBAC attempt, cesareanAntepartum visits + failed VBAC → cesarean + postpartum

Simpler partial-package codes also existed for antepartum-only or delivery-only scenarios:

CodeDescription
59425Antepartum care only — 4 to 6 visits
59426Antepartum care only — 7 or more visits
59430Postpartum care only
59409Vaginal delivery only (no antepartum or postpartum)
59514Cesarean delivery only

How the Payment Model Worked

A payer would contract a single dollar amount for the global package — for example, a flat rate for a "vaginal delivery with prenatal care." The physician received that single payment regardless of how many prenatal visits occurred, how complex the labor was, or what complications arose. One claim, one payment, whole episode.

For your script: A good analogy is an all-inclusive resort price. No matter how much you eat at the restaurant or use the gym, the price doesn't change. Under the new system, every meal is itemized — you pay per service, and what you document determines what you're paid.

Why the Global Package Failed — The Case for Change
The structural problems that made this change inevitable
▼

Five Structural Problems That Built Over 30 Years

Problem 1 — Modern Pregnancy Rarely Involves One Doctor

The global package was designed in an era when one physician followed a patient from first prenatal visit through delivery and postpartum. That model is now the exception, not the rule. Today's OB patients may see:

  • A community OB/GYN for antepartum visits
  • A Maternal-Fetal Medicine specialist for high-risk consultations
  • An OB hospitalist or laborist who manages labor and delivers the baby
  • A different physician for postpartum hospital care and office follow-up

One global code cannot properly compensate four different physicians for their distinct portions of care. The result was complicated shared-billing arrangements, reduced compensation for providers delivering only part of the episode, and claims that didn't reflect what actually happened clinically.

Problem 2 — High-Risk and Complex Care Was Systematically Underpaid

A global code pays the same whether a pregnancy is uncomplicated or involves gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, preterm labor, and three MFM consultations. Complexity was invisible to payers. MFM specialists in particular were chronically underpaid relative to the intensity and volume of services they provided, because their work was either bundled away or captured inadequately by separate consultation codes that payers routinely denied.

Problem 3 — Care Across Multiple Settings Was Not Captured

Modern pregnancy care increasingly involves:

  • Telehealth prenatal visits — accelerated after 2020
  • Home monitoring — fetal heart rate, blood pressure, glucose
  • Hospital observation and readmissions during pregnancy
  • Rural-to-urban transfer when a complication emerges mid-pregnancy

The global package had no mechanism to account for care delivered across these settings or by multiple facilities. It was built for a single-physician, single-setting model.

Problem 4 — Postpartum Care Was Undervalued and Rarely Used

The global package included postpartum care, but this created a perverse incentive: the postpartum visit was "already paid for" and therefore generated no additional revenue, leading to underscheduling of postpartum follow-up at precisely the period when maternal mortality risk is highest. The leading causes of maternal death — hemorrhage, cardiomyopathy, hypertensive disorders, mental health crises — cluster in the first 6–12 weeks postpartum. A payment model that treated postpartum care as a zero-marginal-cost add-on was incompatible with modern maternal health goals.

Problem 5 — Billing and Documentation Were Misaligned

Because the global code paid a flat rate, there was no financial incentive to document encounter complexity at an E/M level. Antepartum notes often lacked the Medical Decision-Making documentation that would support higher-level E/M codes — not because the care wasn't complex, but because it didn't matter under the old model. This created a documentation culture that is now, suddenly, directly relevant to revenue. Physicians who never needed to think about MDM levels now do.

The bottom line for your script: The global package created a payment system where complexity didn't matter, multiple providers couldn't be fairly compensated, and the incentives were wrong for postpartum care. The AMA and ACOG spent nearly two years redesigning this. The result is CPT 2027.

Who Drove the Change — AMA, ACOG & the Advocacy Timeline
The institutional story behind CPT 2027
▼
Why this matters for your script: Physicians will ask "Who decided this?" The answer is their own specialty society — ACOG — in partnership with the AMA. This isn't something imposed by CMS or payers. It's a physician-led redesign.

ACOG's Advocacy Role

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) has been the primary driver of global package reform. ACOG's position, stated formally in their payment advocacy materials, is that the global OB package has been "inadequate to describe modern obstetric care" for years and that unbundling was necessary to achieve fair payment for the full scope of OB services.

ACOG celebrated the April 2026 announcement explicitly, stating: "After years of ACOG advocacy, the AMA has released new obstetric codes." This was a goal the specialty had pursued through multiple CPT revision cycles.

The AMA CPT Editorial Panel Process

CPT codes are owned and maintained by the AMA. Changes require approval by the AMA CPT Editorial Panel, which reviews applications from medical specialty societies. The process for the OB unbundling involved:

  • Nearly two years of collaborative development between AMA editorial staff, ACOG, SMFM (Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine), and other stakeholders
  • Clinical validation of new code definitions and criteria
  • Formal CPT Editorial Panel review and approval
  • Publication of the 2027 CPT code set with new maternity care codes finalized

SMFM's Position

The Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine (SMFM) has also publicly supported the transition, noting the changes will better reflect the complexity of high-risk obstetric care and improve payment equity between general OB/GYN and MFM specialties — a long-standing concern for MFM practices.

Script note: When physicians ask "why are we doing this?" — the answer is: ACOG fought for this. The AMA approved it. This is the profession correcting a 30-year-old payment model that wasn't designed for how we practice today.

The New Framework — Four Phases of Maternity Care
The conceptual architecture of CPT 2027 OB coding
▼
The central idea for your script: The AMA essentially "broke apart" the bundled pregnancy into four distinct, separately billable phases. Each phase has its own codes. Each service within a phase is now individually reported. This is the fundamental shift.
Phase 1
Antepartum Care
Every prenatal visit billed individually using standard E/M office visit codes. Each encounter stands alone.
Phase 2
Labor Management
New dedicated codes billed once per calendar date. Distinguishes straightforward from complex and initial from subsequent days.
Phase 3
Delivery
New stand-alone delivery codes by type (vaginal, VBAC, primary C-section, repeat C-section). Not bundled with labor or postpartum.
Phase 4
Postpartum Care
Inpatient post-delivery days billed with subsequent hospital care E/M codes. Outpatient postpartum visits billed per encounter.

Phase 1 — Antepartum in Detail

Each antepartum office visit is now reported with the standard E/M office visit code appropriate for that encounter. New patient codes run 99202–99205 and established patient codes run 99211–99215. The level selected depends on either the complexity of Medical Decision-Making (MDM) or total encounter time — the same selection methodology already used for all other office E/M visits.

Telehealth prenatal visits are reported with the telehealth E/M range (98000–98015). Inpatient management during pregnancy uses inpatient subsequent hospital care codes (99231–99233). Home visits use home service codes. Critical condition during pregnancy uses critical care codes (99291, 99292).

How many claims does this generate? The ACOG recommendation for routine prenatal visits is approximately 10–14 office visits for a term pregnancy. Under the old model, all those visits were bundled into a single global code. Under the new model, each visit generates a separate claim. For a physician with 100 deliveries per year, this represents a shift from ~100 annual global claims to potentially 1,000–1,400+ annual antepartum claims alone — plus labor management and delivery claims.

Phase 2 — Labor Management in Detail

Labor management is now reported separately from delivery. The physician bills a labor management code for each calendar date on which they provide labor management services — distinct from the delivery itself. Labor management codes have two axes:

  • Initial vs. Subsequent Day: The first calendar date of labor management uses an "initial" code. Each subsequent calendar date uses a "subsequent" code.
  • Straightforward vs. Complex: The complexity level is determined by six AMA-defined criteria (see Section 7). All six must be met for straightforward; one deviation triggers complex.

Phase 3 — Delivery in Detail

The delivery itself is reported with a new stand-alone delivery code. There are separate codes by delivery type: vaginal, VBAC, primary cesarean, repeat cesarean. The delivery code does not include labor management (which is billed separately) and does not include postpartum care (which is billed as subsequent hospital care and/or office E/M visits). Routine same-day immediate postpartum care is included within the delivery code itself.

New codes also exist for third-degree and fourth-degree laceration repair — previously there were no dedicated codes for these, and they were unbillable when performed as part of a global delivery.

Phase 4 — Postpartum in Detail

All dedicated postpartum codes (including 59430) are deleted. Postpartum care is reported as follows:

  • Inpatient postpartum days (day after delivery through discharge): Subsequent hospital care codes per day (99231–99233)
  • Hospital discharge: Discharge management code (99238 for ≤30 minutes, 99239 for >30 minutes)
  • Outpatient postpartum office visits: Office E/M codes (99211–99215) per encounter

This is significant because it makes every postpartum encounter separately reimbursable — creating a direct financial incentive for postpartum follow-up that did not exist under the global model.

Labor Management Rules — The Six Criteria
Straightforward vs. complex — how the determination is made
▼
Why this section matters for your script: The straightforward vs. complex distinction is the most nuanced clinical decision in the new system. Every labor management day requires this determination. Physicians need to understand it cold.

The Six Straightforward Criteria (ALL must be met)

✓ Straightforward — All Six Required

  • Singleton vertex presentation
  • Routine maternal and fetal monitoring
  • Fetal monitoring NOT requiring physician intervention
  • Normal labor progression OR routine induction / augmentation
  • Stable medical conditions — no additional management required
  • No prior cesarean delivery

✗ Complex — Any ONE Triggers This

  • Multiple gestation (twins, triplets, or more)
  • Non-vertex presentation (breech, transverse, oblique)
  • Fetal monitoring requiring physician intervention
  • Abnormal labor progression or labor arrest
  • Deteriorating or unstable medical condition during labor
  • Prior cesarean delivery (VBAC attempt is always complex)

AMA Rule 1 — Same-Day Complexity Upgrade (AMA Confirmed)

Scenario: Labor begins as straightforward on a given calendar date, but during that same day the patient develops a complication — labor arrest, fetal distress requiring intervention, unstable blood pressure — and transitions to complex.

Correct coding: Report ONLY the complex labor management code for that calendar date (59081 for initial day, 59083 for subsequent). Do not report both straightforward and complex on the same day. The AMA has explicitly confirmed this rule in the June 2026 coding primer.

AMA Rule 2 — Planned / Scheduled Cesarean (AMA Confirmed)

Scenario: A patient is scheduled for an elective or planned cesarean with no trial of labor. The physician arrives, performs the C-section, and no labor management services were provided.

Correct coding: Do NOT report any labor management code (59080–59083). Report only the cesarean delivery code. Labor management codes require that labor management services were actually provided. A physician cannot bill for labor management they did not perform simply because the patient was admitted for delivery.

Labor Management vs. E/M — The Critical Distinction

Labor management codes (59080–59083) are not E/M codes. They are procedure codes that cover a defined scope of physician services on the labor and delivery unit for that calendar date. They include:

  • Interim evaluation and physical examination
  • Collection and interpretation of physiological data (fetal monitoring, contraction patterns)
  • Management of induction, augmentation, or spontaneous labor
  • Patient counseling and care coordination on L&D

A physician does not separately bill an E/M code for the routine daily management visit on L&D — the labor management code covers that work. The distinction matters because it avoids double-billing for the same service.

For your script: A useful way to frame it is "one labor management code per calendar day covers everything I did on the floor that day for this patient — monitoring, interpreting, adjusting the plan." If the delivery happens on the same day, the delivery code is added on top.

The Documentation Shift — What Changes for Physicians at the Note Level
Why documentation habits must change and what that looks like
▼
The single biggest behavioral change for physicians: Under the old global model, documentation complexity didn't directly affect payment. Under the new model, every antepartum and postpartum visit E/M code level is directly tied to what the physician documented. Underdocumentation = underpayment or audit exposure.

E/M Level Selection — MDM vs. Time

For every antepartum and postpartum office visit, the physician (or their billing staff) must select an E/M code level. The AMA allows two methods:

  • Medical Decision-Making (MDM): The code level is determined by the complexity of the clinical decisions documented — the number of problems addressed, the amount of data reviewed, and the risk of the management plan.
  • Total Time: The code level is determined by the total time the physician spent on the encounter on that date (including pre- and post-visit work, not just face-to-face time).

The physician may choose whichever method supports the higher code for a given encounter — but the method must be documented. If billing by time, total encounter time must be recorded in the note.

The Audit Risk

Under the global package, post-payment audits focused on whether the delivery happened and the appropriate global code was used — relatively straightforward. Under the new system, every antepartum claim at a higher E/M level (99214, 99215) is a potential audit target. Auditors will look for documentation supporting:

  • The problems addressed at that specific visit
  • The data reviewed and its complexity
  • The management decisions made and their risk level
  • OR the total encounter time if billing by time

A physician who consistently bills 99214 (moderate complexity) for every prenatal visit without documentation supporting that complexity is at elevated audit risk. The EHR template updates being deployed before training are specifically designed to prompt the documentation elements needed to support these code levels.

For your script: The message isn't "document more to bill more." The message is: "You were already providing complex care — now your documentation needs to reflect that so you get paid for the work you were already doing."

Modifier TH may not be the route we implement. Do not include Modifier TH guidance in the training scripts until you receive explicit direction from the project team. Background context is provided below for reference only — so you understand what it is and why it exists — but leave it out of the modules for now. What Modifier TH Is (Background Reference Only)

HCPCS Level II modifier TH stands for "Obstetrical treatment/services, prenatal or postpartum." It is a two-character code appended to the E/M code on the claim line to signal to the payer that this E/M visit is for obstetric/prenatal/postpartum care — differentiating it from a general medicine office visit billed by the same practice under the same code.

Why the AMA and ACOG Discussed It

Because antepartum and postpartum visits under the new system use the same E/M codes (99202–99215) as any other office visit, payers needed a mechanism to identify which claims were part of a pregnancy episode. Modifier TH was proposed as that identifier, allowing payers to:

  • Apply the correct fee schedule rates negotiated for OB services (which may differ from general office visit rates)
  • Track pregnancy episode claims for data and audit purposes
  • Differentiate for any payer-specific OB-related policies (prior authorization, global episode flags, etc.)

The Complexity (Why Our Approach Is Under Review)

Modifier TH implementation is not as simple as appending two characters to a claim. It involves:

  • EHR and billing system configuration to automatically apply or flag the modifier
  • Verification that each contracted payer accepts and correctly processes TH
  • Staff training on when to apply vs. omit it
  • Risk of claim delays or denials if payers have varying implementations

Our organization is evaluating whether an alternative claim-level identifier, a different internal workflow, or a payer-specific approach better fits our systems. A decision will be made before the training modules are finalized.

Because CMS doesn't finalize RVU values until November 2026, we cannot tell physicians exact dollar amounts for the new codes during training. This is normal for any CPT transition — code definitions are finalized before CMS assigns values. The important message for the script is:

  • The code structure and clinical criteria are finalized and certain
  • Reimbursement rates will be confirmed in November 2026 — before go-live
  • ACOG has publicly stated that the new codes are expected to provide better payment for complex and high-risk care compared to the global package
  • The clinical and documentation requirements do not depend on knowing the RVU values

Talking Points — Anticipating Physician Pushback
The questions your modules will need to answer
▼
For your script: Physicians are skeptical by nature and will arrive at training with objections. The best modules pre-empt the top questions rather than waiting for the Q&A. These are the ones you'll hear most.

"Why is this happening to us?"

Script answer: This isn't something imposed on OB. ACOG advocated for this change for years. The global package was designed in the early 1990s for a one-doctor, one-office model. Modern obstetrics — with hospitalists, MFM specialists, telehealth, and multi-site practices — outgrew it. ACOG and the AMA worked together to build a system that actually reflects how we practice today and pays appropriately for complex care.

"This is going to create a lot more paperwork."

Script answer: Documentation habits need to change — but it's not about writing more. It's about writing what you were already doing. When you managed a patient with gestational diabetes, adjusted insulin, ordered additional monitoring, and coordinated with MFM — that's moderate-to-high complexity E/M work. You were doing it. You just weren't documenting it at the level that supports the code. The new templates are designed to make it easier to capture that work without adding significant time to your note.

"What if I just keep billing the old codes?"

Script answer: The old codes don't exist after January 1, 2027. There is nothing to bill. The codes are deleted from the CPT code set entirely. A claim submitted with 59400 in 2027 will be rejected — not denied for medical necessity, not appealed — simply rejected because the code doesn't exist. There is no path forward with the old codes.

"Will we get paid less under this system?"

Script answer: That depends directly on how well you document. For routine uncomplicated care, revenue should be comparable. For high-complexity care — high-risk pregnancies, complicated labors, complex medical management — the new system should produce better reimbursement because complexity is now visible to payers in a way the global package never allowed. Physicians who document well stand to benefit from the new model. Final CMS RVU values will be published in November 2026 before any 2027 claims are submitted.

"I don't understand the labor management criteria."

Script answer: The AMA defined six criteria for "straightforward" labor management. Think of it as describing an uncomplicated labor: singleton vertex, normal monitoring, no interventions needed, normal progression, no prior cesarean, stable mom. If your patient meets all six — straightforward. If anything on that list is off — complex. Most practicing OBs will tell you that truly straightforward labor is the exception in their actual caseload, which is exactly why the AMA created a complex category with a higher-value code.

"What about patients who see multiple providers during their pregnancy?"

Script answer: This is actually one of the problems the new system was designed to solve. Under the old global package, split-care arrangements were a billing headache — only one provider could bill the global code, and the "split" had to be negotiated between practices. Under the new system, each provider bills for the services they personally provided. The antepartum physician bills for the antepartum visits. The hospitalist bills for labor management. The MFM bills for the specialist consultations. Each physician bills their actual work. The system is now built for how modern OB is actually delivered.

"What if the payers aren't ready?"

Script answer: The AMA published a Health Plan Primer simultaneously with the physician materials in June 2026. ACOG has formally recommended that health plans have new per-code rates in place by September 1, 2026. Our HP team is leading payer outreach now to ensure contracted rates are in place before go-live. January 1, 2027 is a federal CPT effective date — payers are not waiting to update their systems any more than providers are.
