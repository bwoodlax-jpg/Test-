Welcome to Module 1 of the CPT 2027 OB Unbundling training series. This is a resource developed by our coding consultation team to help OB/GYN physicians prepare for one of the most significant changes to maternity care coding in over 30 years. Effective January 1, 2027, the AMA is restructuring the entire CPT maternity care framework. The old global package — one code that bundled all your prenatal visits, the delivery, and the postpartum visit — is gone. In its place: individual, separately reportable codes for each phase of care. This module covers antepartum office visits. We're going to walk through exactly which codes you'll use, how to select the right level, how to apply the correct ICD-10 diagnosis, and run through four real patient scenarios so you know exactly what to do when January 1 arrives.
1
WHAT'S CHANGING — THE BIG PICTURE
~1:30 · ~175 words
▼
📊 Slide: Show "Before vs. After" visual. Highlight deleted codes in red.
Let's start with the big picture. Right now, when a patient delivers, you or your billing team submits one of the global OB codes. For a vaginal delivery with full care, that's 59400. For cesarean, 59510. If someone else delivers and you only handled antepartum, you use 59425 for 4 to 6 visits, or 59426 for 7 or more. One code. Everything bundled. Starting January 1, 2027, those codes are deleted. The full list: 59400, 59409, 59410, 59425, 59426, 59430 — the vaginal global codes — and 59510, 59514, 59515 for cesarean, along with all the delivery-after-previous-cesarean codes. Gone. What replaces them? A completely unbundled framework. Antepartum visits — each one individually — gets its own E/M code. Labor management gets new separate codes. Delivery gets its own codes. Postpartum gets E/M codes. This module is focused entirely on that first phase: antepartum office visits.

59425
Antepartum care only; 4–6 visits (DELETED)
59426

59430

THE NEW CODING FRAMEWORK — E/M FOR EVERY VISIT
~2:00 · ~240 words
▼
📊 Slide: E/M code table. Highlight 99202–99215 prominently.
So what are we using instead? Starting January 1st, antepartum care is reported with standard Evaluation and Management — E/M — codes. These are the same codes you already use for regular office visits. The AMA is explicit: each antepartum encounter is reported with the appropriate E/M code based on where the patient is seen. For your typical prenatal office visit, that means: — For a new patient: codes 99202 through 99205 — For an established patient: codes 99211 through 99215 The fundamental shift is this: instead of counting prenatal visits and rolling them into 59425 or 59426, you are now billing each visit individually. Every prenatal appointment is a standalone claim. And the setting matters. For patients who need inpatient management during pregnancy, use 99221 through 99223 for the initial encounter, or 99231 through 99233 for subsequent hospital days. Telehealth visits use the telehealth E/M range, 98000 through 98015. The good news: these are codes you already know. The framework is familiar. The difference is you're now applying them to every prenatal encounter, not rolling them into a bundle.
Antepartum E/M Codes Effective January 1, 2027
99202–99205
New patient office/outpatient visit — use for first prenatal visit with new patient
99211–99215
Established patient office/outpatient visit — use for all subsequent prenatal visits
99221–99223
Initial hospital inpatient or observation — antepartum inpatient admission
99231–99233
Subsequent hospital care — continued antepartum inpatient management
98000–98015
Telehealth services — antepartum telemedicine visits

HOW TO SELECT THE RIGHT E/M LEVEL — MDM
~2:30 · ~300 words
▼
📊 Slide: MDM 3-element table. Emphasize "Problems Addressed" box.
Now let's talk about how you select the right code for each visit. Most physicians will select by Medical Decision Making — MDM. MDM has three elements: the problems addressed, the amount and complexity of data reviewed, and the risk of complications. Here's the AMA's specific guidance for prenatal visits: pregnancy itself is evaluated under "Problems Addressed." The complexity of that pregnancy is what drives the level. Straightforward to Low — code 99212 or 99213. This is your routine prenatal visit. A patient at 20 weeks, uncomplicated singleton pregnancy, normal vitals, fetal heart tones present, labs reviewed and normal, no new concerns. Moderate complexity — code 99214. This is your patient with a managed complication. Think 28 weeks, well-controlled gestational hypertension. You review blood pressure trends, adjust her monitoring plan. High complexity — 99215. This is your high-risk prenatal visit. Think 32 weeks with poorly controlled gestational diabetes, new concern for intrauterine growth restriction. You're reviewing imaging data, making multiple management changes, coordinating with MFM. The documentation message: every note needs to stand on its own. Document why you made the decision you made.
4
MODIFIER TH + ICD-10-CM CODES
~1:30 · ~180 words
▼
📊 Slide: Modifier TH callout box. Then ICD-10 categories Z34 and O-codes.
Two more elements every antepartum claim needs: Modifier TH and the right ICD-10 code. Modifier TH stands for "Obstetrical treatment or services, antepartum or postpartum." Apply Modifier TH to your prenatal and postpartum E/M claims. This tells the payer: this is a pregnancy-related service. For ICD-10, the two main categories are: Z34.XX for supervision of a normal pregnancy, and the O00 through O9A range for any condition that affects the management of pregnancy. When there's a complication or high-risk condition, use the specific O-code rather than the Z34. Document and code the actual diagnosis.
Key ICD-10-CM Codes for Antepartum Visits
Z34.XX
Encounter for supervision of normal pregnancy (specify trimester and parity)
O00–O9A
Conditions affecting pregnancy management — use specific code for complications
Z33.1
Pregnant state, incidental — only when pregnancy is NOT affecting the encounter
Z3A.XX
Weeks of gestation — assign to specify gestational age

TRANSITION YEAR — PATIENTS WHO SPAN 2026 AND 2027
~1:30 · ~175 words
▼
📊 Slide: Timeline graphic — 2026 visits vs. 2027 visits.
Before we get to the coding examples, there's one scenario your billing team needs to plan for now: patients who have prenatal visits in both 2026 and 2027. Antepartum visits that occur in 2026 are reported using the old codes — 59425 for 4 to 6 visits, 59426 for 7 or more visits — or individual E/M codes if there are fewer than 4 visits in 2026. Antepartum visits that occur in 2027 are reported per-visit with E/M codes. This is a billing team planning item — make sure the date split is built into your workflow before December 31.

FOUR CODING SCENARIOS — PUTTING IT TOGETHER
~2:30 · ~300 words
▼
📊 Slide: Show each scenario card one at a time.
Let's put this all together with four real-world scenarios.
SCENARIO 1 — Routine 28-Week Established Visit
Established OB patient, 28 weeks, uncomplicated singleton pregnancy. Normal vitals, fetal heart tones confirmed. Labs reviewed, all within normal limits. No complaints.
→ 99212 or 99213 | ICD-10: Z34.22 | Modifier TH
SCENARIO 2 — New Patient, First OB Visit at 9 Weeks
New patient, first prenatal visit at 9 weeks. Complete history and physical, prior miscarriage reviewed, initial prenatal lab panel ordered, counseling on diet and activity.
→ 99204 or 99205 | ICD-10: Z34.01 | Modifier TH
SCENARIO 3 — High-Risk Visit, 32 Weeks
Established patient, 32 weeks, type 1 diabetes and gestational hypertension. Glucose logs reviewed, insulin adjusted, BPP reviewed with mild growth restriction concern, MFM referral placed.
→ 99215 | ICD-10: O24.012 + O10.412 + Z3A.32 | Modifier TH
SCENARIO 4 — Telehealth Visit at 36 Weeks
Established patient, 36 weeks, uncomplicated. Telehealth check-in. Kick count log reviewed, birth plan discussed, no acute concerns.
→ 98000 or appropriate telehealth E/M | ICD-10: Z34.33 | Modifier TH
Notice the pattern: each visit has its own E/M code, its own ICD-10 diagnosis, and Modifier TH. That's the new standard for every antepartum encounter.
