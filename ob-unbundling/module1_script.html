Welcome to Module 1 of the CPT 2027 OB Unbundling training series. This is a resource developed by our coding consultation team to help OB/GYN physicians prepare for one of the most significant changes to maternity care coding in over 30 years. Effective January 1, 2027, the AMA is restructuring the entire CPT maternity care framework. The old global package — one code that bundled all your prenatal visits, the delivery, and the postpartum visit — is gone. In its place: individual, separately reportable codes for each phase of care. This module covers antepartum office visits. We're going to walk through exactly which codes you'll use, how to select the right level, how to apply the correct ICD-10 diagnosis, and run through four real patient scenarios so you know exactly what to do when January 1 arrives.
1
WHAT'S CHANGING — THE BIG PICTURE
~1:30 · ~175 words
▼
📊 Slide: Show "Before vs. After" visual. Highlight deleted codes in red.
Let's start with the big picture. Right now, when a patient delivers, you or your billing team submits one of the global OB codes. For a vaginal delivery with full care, that's 59400. For cesarean, 59510. If someone else delivers and you only handled antepartum, you use 59425 for 4 to 6 visits, or 59426 for 7 or more. One code. Everything bundled. Starting January 1, 2027, those codes are deleted. The full list: 59400, 59409, 59410, 59425, 59426, 59430 — the vaginal global codes — and 59510, 59514, 59515 for cesarean, along with all the delivery-after-previous-cesarean codes. Gone. What replaces them? A completely unbundled framework. Antepartum visits — each one individually — gets its own E/M code. Labor management gets new separate codes. Delivery gets its own codes. Postpartum gets E/M codes. This module is focused entirely on that first phase: antepartum office visits.

59400
Routine obstetric care — vaginal delivery global package (DELETED)
59425
Antepartum care only; 4–6 visits (DELETED)
59426
Antepartum care only; 7 or more visits (DELETED)
59430
Postpartum care only (DELETED)

THE NEW CODING FRAMEWORK — E/M FOR EVERY VISIT
~2:00 · ~240 words
▼
📊 Slide: E/M code table. Highlight 99202–99215 prominently.
So what are we using instead? Starting January 1st, antepartum care is reported with standard Evaluation and Management — E/M — codes. These are the same codes you already use for regular office visits. The AMA is explicit: each antepartum encounter is reported with the appropriate E/M code based on where the patient is seen. For your typical prenatal office visit, that means: — For a new patient: codes 99202 through 99205 — For an established patient: codes 99211 through 99215 The fundamental shift is this: instead of counting prenatal visits and rolling them into 59425 or 59426, you are now billing each visit individually. Every prenatal appointment is a standalone claim. And the setting matters. For patients who need inpatient management during pregnancy — hospital admissions, observation stays — use 99221 through 99223 for the initial encounter, or 99231 through 99233 for subsequent hospital days. Telehealth visits use the telehealth E/M range, 98000 through 98015. Home visits use the home service codes. If a patient is in critical condition during pregnancy, 99291 and 99292 apply. The good news: these are codes you already know. The framework is familiar. The difference is you're now applying them to every prenatal encounter, not rolling them into a bundle.
Antepartum E/M Codes Effective January 1, 2027
99202–99205
New patient office/outpatient visit — use for first prenatal visit with new patient
99211–99215
Established patient office/outpatient visit — use for all subsequent prenatal visits
99221–99223
Initial hospital inpatient or observation — antepartum inpatient admission
99231–99233
Subsequent hospital care — continued antepartum inpatient management
98000–98015
Telehealth services — antepartum telemedicine visits

HOW TO SELECT THE RIGHT E/M LEVEL — MDM
~2:30 · ~300 words
▼
📊 Slide: MDM 3-element table. Emphasize "Problems Addressed" box. Show 99212–99215 complexity ladder.
Now let's talk about how you select the right code for each visit. Most physicians will select by Medical Decision Making — MDM. MDM has three elements: the problems addressed, the amount and complexity of data reviewed, and the risk of complications. Here's the AMA's specific guidance for prenatal visits: pregnancy itself is evaluated under "Problems Addressed." The complexity of that pregnancy is what drives the level. A routine uncomplicated pregnancy at a low-complexity visit looks different from a patient with gestational diabetes, hypertension, and a growth concern. Let me give you three quick levels as a reference point. Straightforward to Low — code 99212 or 99213. This is your routine prenatal visit. A patient at 20 weeks, uncomplicated singleton pregnancy, normal vitals, fetal heart tones present, labs reviewed and normal, no new concerns. The problem is stable, minimal data review, minimal risk. That's 99212 or 99213 depending on how you document complexity. Moderate complexity — code 99214. This is your patient with a managed complication. Think 28 weeks, well-controlled gestational hypertension. You review blood pressure trends, adjust her monitoring plan, discuss warning signs with her. Multiple data points reviewed, management decision made. That's 99214. High complexity — 99215. This is your high-risk prenatal visit. Think 32 weeks with poorly controlled gestational diabetes, new concern for intrauterine growth restriction on today's ultrasound. You're reviewing imaging data, making multiple management changes, coordinating with MFM. That's 99215. The documentation message: every note needs to stand on its own. Document why you made the decision you made. Under the old global model, a visit note just confirmed the visit happened. Under the new model, the note has to support the specific E/M level billed. The AMA's own language is: documentation to support E/M code selection is key, particularly on Risk.
4
MODIFIER TH + ICD-10-CM CODES
~1:30 · ~180 words
▼
📊 Slide: Modifier TH callout box. Then ICD-10 categories Z34 and O-codes.
Two more elements every antepartum claim needs: Modifier TH and the right ICD-10 code. Modifier TH stands for "Obstetrical treatment or services, antepartum or postpartum." The AMA confirms you should apply Modifier TH to your prenatal and postpartum E/M claims. This tells the payer: this is a pregnancy-related service. Without it, some payers may process these like a standard office visit and deny or underpay. Apply TH to every antepartum E/M claim. For ICD-10, the two main categories are: Z34.XX, which covers encounters for supervision of a normal pregnancy, and the O00 through O9A range for any condition that affects the management of pregnancy. The specific Z34 code is based on trimester and whether it's the patient's first pregnancy — for example Z34.01 for first trimester of a first pregnancy, Z34.22 for second trimester of a subsequent pregnancy. When there's a complication or high-risk condition, use the specific O-code rather than the Z34. Document and code the actual diagnosis.
Key ICD-10-CM Codes for Antepartum Visits
Z34.XX
Encounter for supervision of normal pregnancy (specify trimester and parity)
O00–O9A
Conditions affecting pregnancy management — use specific code for complications
Z33.1
Pregnant state, incidental — only when pregnancy is NOT affecting the encounter
Z3A.XX
Weeks of gestation — assign to specify gestational age (use completed weeks)

TRANSITION YEAR — PATIENTS WHO SPAN 2026 AND 2027
~1:30 · ~175 words
▼
📊 Slide: Timeline graphic — 2026 visits vs. 2027 visits. Highlight the December/January split point.
Before we get to the coding examples, there's one scenario your billing team needs to plan for now: patients who have prenatal visits in both 2026 and 2027. If a patient starts her prenatal care in mid-2026, some visits happen under the old rules — and she delivers in 2027 under the new ones. The AMA's guidance is clear on how to handle the split. Antepartum visits that occur in 2026 are reported using the old codes — 59425 for 4 to 6 visits, 59426 for 7 or more visits — or individual E/M codes if there are fewer than 4 visits in 2026. Antepartum visits that occur in 2027 are reported per-visit with E/M codes. The AMA's planning guidance: pregnancies with first visits in January through May of 2026 will likely complete under the current global framework. Pregnancies starting in September 2026 or later will have 3 or fewer prenatal visits in 2026, meaning E/M codes apply. This is a billing team planning item — make sure the date split is built into your workflow before December 31.

FOUR CODING SCENARIOS — PUTTING IT TOGETHER
~2:30 · ~300 words
▼
📊 Slide: Show each scenario card one at a time. Walk through codes on screen. Pause between scenarios.
Let's put this all together with four real-world scenarios. I'll give you the clinical situation, and then the complete code set you'd report.
SCENARIO 1 — Routine 28-Week Established Visit
Established OB patient, 28 weeks, uncomplicated singleton pregnancy. Normal vitals, fetal heart tones confirmed. You review labs from the prior week — all within normal limits. Patient has no complaints. Brief discussion on upcoming glucose screening. Straightforward management, minimal data.
→ 99212 or 99213 | ICD-10: Z34.22 (supervision of normal pregnancy, second trimester) | Modifier TH
Note: 99212 if straightforward documentation; 99213 if low complexity with data review documented.
SCENARIO 2 — New Patient, First OB Visit at 9 Weeks
New patient presenting for her first prenatal visit at 9 weeks. You perform a complete history and physical, review prior medical history including a prior miscarriage, order the initial prenatal lab panel, and counsel on diet, activity restrictions, and warning signs.
→ 99204 or 99205 | ICD-10: Z34.01 (first trimester, first pregnancy) | Modifier TH
Note: 99205 if history complexity and counseling time support high complexity documentation.
SCENARIO 3 — High-Risk Visit, 32 Weeks
Established patient at 32 weeks with type 1 diabetes and gestational hypertension. You review glucose logs, adjust insulin regimen, review today's BPP results showing concern for mild growth restriction, discuss additional monitoring plan, and send a referral to maternal-fetal medicine.
→ 99215 | ICD-10: O24.012 (diabetes in pregnancy, type 1) + O10.412 (hypertension in pregnancy) + Z3A.32 | Modifier TH
Note: Multiple problems, independent review of imaging, management decisions including referral = high complexity MDM.
SCENARIO 4 — Telehealth Visit at 36 Weeks
Established patient, 36 weeks, uncomplicated pregnancy. Patient connects via telehealth for routine check-in. You review kick count log, discuss birth plan preferences, confirm next appointment. Brief data review, no acute concerns, no management changes.
→ 98000 or appropriate telehealth E/M code | ICD-10: Z34.33 (supervision of normal pregnancy, third trimester) | Modifier TH
Note: Verify your payer's telehealth coverage policy — some require specific platform or consent documentation.
Notice the pattern across all four: each visit has its own E/M code, its own ICD-10 diagnosis, and Modifier TH. That's the new standard for every antepartum encounter.

Question 1 of 6
Under CPT 2027, antepartum office visits are reported using which type of code?
✓ Correct answer: B — Each antepartum visit is reported with the appropriate E/M code based on the location and complexity of the encounter.
Question 2 of 6
Which modifier should be applied to antepartum and postpartum E/M claims under CPT 2027?
✓ Correct answer: C — Modifier TH identifies the claim as a pregnancy-related service and is recommended by the AMA for all antepartum and postpartum E/M visits.
Question 3 of 6
An established patient is seen at 28 weeks with well-controlled gestational hypertension. You review BP trends, adjust her monitoring plan, and counsel her on warning signs. Which E/M level most likely applies?
✓ Correct answer: D — 99214. Gestational hypertension is a chronic condition with exacerbation potential, data is reviewed, and a management plan is modified. That supports moderate MDM complexity.
Question 4 of 6
For a patient whose prenatal care begins in August 2026 and delivers in February 2027, how should antepartum visits be reported?
✓ Correct answer: B — Per AMA guidance, antepartum visits occurring in 2026 follow 2026 CPT rules (59425/59426 or E/M for fewer than 4 visits); visits in 2027 are reported per-visit with E/M codes.
Question 5 of 6
Which ICD-10-CM code category covers encounters for supervision of a normal pregnancy?
✓ Correct answer: C — Z34.XX covers supervision of normal pregnancy (specify trimester and parity). O00–O9A is used when there are complications. Z3A is used to report weeks of gestation as an additional code.
Question 6 of 6
Under CPT 2027 MDM rules, where does pregnancy factor into code level selection?
✓ Correct answer: C — Per AMA CPT 2027 guidance, pregnancy as a condition is evaluated under "Problems Addressed" in the MDM framework. The complexity of that pregnancy determines the appropriate level.
